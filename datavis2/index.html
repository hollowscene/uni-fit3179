GLOBAL CARBON EMISSIONS: EXPONENTIAL RISE

Climate change is one of the most pressing issues of the current century. Key findings by the United Nations have shown that human activities, primarily the burning of fossil fuels, have been the main driver for climate change. Climate change affects everyone. Rapidly rising temperatures, increased frequency of natural disasters and disruption to natural fauna and flora pose dire risks for countries across the world.
And yet, despite all this warning, global carbon emissions are still on the rise. The chart below shows that yearly carbon emissions are beginning to slow down in North America and Europe but these gains are being offset by increases of carbon emissions in Asia predominantly over the last three decades.

CARBON EMISSIONS PER CAPITA

But, looking at absolute carbon emissions doesn't tell the entire story. It's only natural that more populated nations need to produce more carbon emissions. Instead, a fairer way to analyse global carbon emissions is to look at the per capita emissions.
On a global scale there is still a clear disparity between the carbon emissions produced by different countries. This map below shows the per capita CO2 emissions produced by every country. Use the slider to adjust the year being shown.

Inequality And Negligence

INCOME INEQUALITY

One glaring pattern from looking at per capita carbon emissions is that the countries emitting more are often first-world countries.

If we group countries into income brackets its clear to see that poorer countries contribute substantially less carbon emissions to the planet than richer countries. This is a looming ethical issue as the biggest problem is that poorer countries will find it harder for themselves to prevent and recover from the consequences of climate change.

THE WORST EMITTERS

And among these rich countries is an even more glaring issue. So we know that CO2 emissions are largely driven by richer countries. Even worse than that, the top emitters of 2020 were a small subset of countries with tiny populations but massive carbon emissions. New Caledonia (30.45 Tonnes per capita) Qatar (37.02 Tonnes per capita) Mongolia (26.98 Tonnes per capita) Trinidad and Tobago (25.37 Tonnes per capita)

These are a distinct class of countries that have tiny populations but have absolutely massive CO2 contributions per capita due to large mining or oil based economies and reducing the carbon emissions in these countries should be a priority.
