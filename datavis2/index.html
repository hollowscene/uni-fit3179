GLOBAL CARBON EMISSIONS: EXPONENTIAL RISE

Climate change is one of the most pressing issues of the current century. Key findings by the United Nations have shown that human activities, primarily the burning of fossil fuels, are the main driver for climate change. Climate change affects everyone. Rapidly rising temperatures, increased frequency of natural disasters and disruption to natural fauna and flora pose dire risks for the entire planet.
And yet, despite all this warning, global carbon emissions are still on the rise. The chart below shows that yearly carbon emissions are beginning to slow down in North America and Europe but these gains are being offset by increases of carbon emissions in Asia predominantly over the last three decades.

CARBON EMISSIONS PER CAPITA

But, looking at absolute carbon emissions doesn't tell the entire story. It's only natural that more populated nations need to produce more carbon emissions. Instead, a fairer way to assess global carbon emissions is to look at emissions per capita.
On a global scale there is a clear disparity between the carbon emissions produced by different countries. The map below shows the per capita CO2 emissions produced by every country. Use the slider to adjust the year being shown.

Inequality And Negligence

INCOME INEQUALITY

One glaring pattern from looking at per capita carbon emissions is that the countries emitting more are often first-world countries.

The chart on the right shows the mean carbon emissions per capita for countries grouped by income in 2020. Poorer countries contribute substantially less carbon emissions to the planet than richer countries. This is a looming problem as these poorer countries lack the financial resources to prevent and recover from the consequences of climate change.

THE WORST EMITTERS

While CO2 emissions are largely driven by richer countries, its not always the case. The chart on the left shows the top 10 worst emitting countries in any particular year. In recent decades, the worst emitters are often lower-middle or upper-middle income countries who rely on carbon intensive industries such as coal mining.

This is a huge political issue. These countries have economies that are overly reliant on these carbon intensive industries which means they are often unable to reduce emissions without significant impacts to their economies. But on the other hand it sets a bad example for even poorer nations who may question why these other countries get a free pass.

THE GROWING MIDDLE CLASS

While the proportion of carbon emissions from high-income countries is decreasing, its mostly driven by a sharp rise in carbon emissions emitted by middle-income countries. This is especially the case for cement and coal which are largely driven by rapidly developing countries like India and China.

This will be an important consideration for policy makers. As the lives of people in more and more countries improves, so to will the carbon emissions they emit increase.

NO EASY SOLUTION

What Can You Do?

The unfortunate reality is that as an individual, you really can't do that much to stop climate change. Small individual sacrifices such as taking public transport or eating less meat are beneficial, but will only make a small dent in the massive amounts of carbon emissions produced on a globl scale.

But, its not all doom and gloom. Recent global commitments such as the Paris Agreement and Net Zero by 2050 are encouraging steps forward. Recent increases in the frequency of natural disasters have also placed climate change at the front of many peoples' minds. The fight against climate change will be a collective one and it will especially require the efforts of leaders of the most well-off nations.

The most important thing you can do in the fight against climate change is to be informed about the science and to ensure that those you know are also informed. The United Nations and the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) are two global organisations that are major contributors to the fight against climate change.
